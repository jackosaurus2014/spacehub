WHY THIS IS #1 PRIORITY
------------------------

Every single competitor in the space intelligence market has company profiles
as a core feature:

  - Quilty Space: M&A tracker, company profiles, satellite operator database
  - BryceTech: Government contractor database, startup ecosystem mapping
  - Seradata/Slingshot: SpaceTrak operator profiles (60+ years of data)
  - Novaspace (Euroconsult): Company profiles across their market research
  - Payload Pro: Curated company analysis and deal flow coverage
  - Crunchbase/PitchBook: General company data with some space coverage

SpaceNexus has 44 modules, 26 API integrations, and comprehensive market
data -- but ZERO company profiles. When an enterprise prospect asks "what
can you tell me about [Rocket Lab / L3Harris / Axiom Space]?" we have
nothing. This is a deal-breaker for enterprise sales.

The Company Intelligence Database is table stakes. Without it, we are a
dashboard with no substance. With it, we become a platform worth paying for.


DATA MODEL MVP (What Each Company Profile Needs)
-------------------------------------------------

Based on analysis of PitchBook, Crunchbase, CB Insights, and Quilty's
models, here is the recommended schema for space company profiles:

BASIC INFORMATION
  - Company name, legal name, ticker symbol (if public)
  - Founded date, HQ location, employee count (range)
  - Company type: Launcher, Satellite Manufacturer, Operator, Ground Segment,
    Data/Analytics, Services, Component Supplier, Launch Services
  - Sub-sector tags (multiple): LEO, GEO, MEO, Cislunar, Deep Space, etc.
  - Status: Active, Acquired, Merged, Defunct, Pre-Revenue, IPO'd
  - Website, LinkedIn URL, X/Twitter URL

FINANCIAL DATA
  - Total funding raised (amount, currency, last updated date)
  - Funding rounds: date, type (Seed/A/B/C/etc.), amount, lead investors
  - Revenue (if public or reported): annual, quarterly
  - Valuation (if known): last round, public market cap
  - M&A activity: acquirer/target, date, amount, status

KEY PEOPLE
  - CEO, CTO, CFO, COO + title, LinkedIn URL
  - Board members (names, affiliations)
  - Key hires and departures (last 12 months)

SPACE-SPECIFIC DATA (This is our unique differentiator)
  - Orbital assets: satellites in orbit (name, NORAD ID, orbit type, status)
  - Launch manifest: upcoming launches (date, vehicle, payload, orbit)
  - Launch history: past launches (date, vehicle, outcome)
  - Spectrum licenses: FCC/ITU filings (frequency bands, status)
  - Government contracts: SAM.gov awards (agency, value, date, type)
  - FAA launch/reentry licenses
  - ITAR/export control relevance
  - Patents: count, key patent areas

RELATIONSHIPS
  - Competitors (manual curation initially)
  - Partners and suppliers
  - Customers (if publicly known)
  - Investors

CONTENT/NEWS
  - Recent news articles (auto-linked from our RSS feed aggregation)
  - Recent AI Insights mentioning the company
  - SpaceNexus module data referencing the company

METADATA
  - Data freshness: last_verified timestamp per field
  - Data source attribution per field
  - Edit history (for future crowdsourcing)

DATA SOURCING STRATEGY
-----------------------

FREE SOURCES (available immediately):

  1. SEC EDGAR: Public filings for listed companies (SpaceX is private but
     Spire, Planet, AST, etc. file). 10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, proxy statements.
     Automate with EDGAR API.

  2. FCC ULS/IBFS: Spectrum license filings, earth station registrations,
     experimental licenses. SpaceX Starlink, OneWeb, Kuiper all file here.
     Searchable via FCC API.

  3. FAA Launch Licenses: All US launch operators must have FAA/AST licenses.
     Publicly available. Updated quarterly.

  4. SAM.gov: Federal contract awards. Search by NAICS codes for space
     (336414 - Guided Missiles, 336415 - Space Vehicle Propulsion,
     334511 - Search & Detection, 517410 - Satellite Telecom).
     SAM.gov has a public API.

  5. CelesTrak/Space-Track: Satellite catalog with NORAD IDs, orbital
     elements. Cross-reference to companies for "orbital assets" field.
     We already integrate CelesTrak.

  6. Crunchbase Basic API: Free tier gives basic company data, funding
     rounds, and team info for many space startups. Rate-limited but usable.

  7. Our Own RSS/News Feed: We aggregate 53 RSS feeds and 39 blog sources.
     NLP extraction of company mentions from article text can auto-populate
     the "recent news" section of each company profile.

  8. LinkedIn Company Pages: Manual data collection for employee count
     ranges, key people. Not automatable without LinkedIn Sales Navigator.

  9. Wikipedia/Wikidata: Surprisingly good for founding dates, HQ locations,
     and basic company info on established players.

  10. Company Press Releases: GlobeNewsWire, BusinessWire, PR Newswire
      feeds for funding announcements, contract wins, product launches.

SEMI-AUTOMATED (AI-assisted, weeks 3-6):

  1. AI Entity Extraction: Run Claude over our news article corpus to
     extract company mentions, funding amounts, contract values, and
     personnel changes. Map to company profiles automatically.

  2. RSS Feed Company Monitoring: Set up per-company keyword alerts
     across our 53 RSS sources. Auto-tag articles to company profiles.

  3. Government Contract Monitoring: Daily SAM.gov API pulls for new
     awards in space NAICS codes. Auto-match to company profiles.

MANUAL (human-curated, ongoing):

  1. Competitive positioning analysis (per company)
  2. Key personnel changes (verified)
  3. Strategic analysis and commentary
  4. Relationship mapping (competitors, partners)
  5. Data verification and freshness audits

PAID SOURCES (future, when revenue supports it):

  1. PitchBook API ($2,500-$5,000/month): Comprehensive funding data,
     valuations, investor details. Not justified until $50K+ MRR.

  2. S&P Capital IQ: Deep financial data on public companies. Expensive
     ($2K+/month per seat). Consider when enterprise customers demand it.

  3. Crunchbase Enterprise: $49/month gets better API access, more data
     fields. Worth it immediately.

  *** CRITICAL FINDING FROM DEEP SEO AUDIT ***

  57 OF 68 PAGE FILES ARE 'use client' COMPONENTS WITH NO METADATA.
  In Next.js App Router, client components cannot export static metadata.
  This means 57 module pages (including /satellites, /market-intel, /news,
  /mission-cost, /compliance, /business-opportunities) ALL FALL BACK to
  the generic root layout title: "SpaceNexus - Space Industry Intelligence
  Platform." Google sees 57 pages with IDENTICAL titles and descriptions.
  This is the single biggest technical SEO problem and must be fixed first.

  FIX: For each module page, create a server-component wrapper (layout.tsx
  or parent server component) that exports metadata, then renders the
  client component as a child. Estimated effort: 2-3 days of dev work.

  ALSO: The StructuredData component has an AggregateRating claiming 4.8/5
  from 150 reviews. This appears fabricated. Google's guidelines prohibit
  self-generated review markup. If detected, could trigger a manual
  penalty. Remove immediately unless backed by verifiable third-party reviews.

  *** END CRITICAL FINDING ***

THE BLOOMBERG MODEL (Inspiration, Not Literal Comparison)
----------------------------------------------------------

Bloomberg Terminal by the Numbers:
  - Founded: 1981 by Michael Bloomberg
  - First customer: Merrill Lynch (22 terminals)
  - 2025-2026 revenue: ~$13-14 billion/year (Bloomberg LP)
  - Terminal subscribers: ~335,000+ globally
  - Price: ~$24,000/year per terminal ($2,000/month)
  - Employees: ~20,000+
  - Revenue per employee: ~$650,000

Why Bloomberg Won:
  1. IT BECAME A UTILITY: You literally cannot work in finance without it.
     Bloomberg Anywhere is the first app opened and last app closed.
  2. MESSAGING LOCK-IN: Bloomberg Chat/IB messaging created a closed network
     of 335,000 finance professionals. The social graph is the moat.
  3. DATA + TOOLS + WORKFLOW: Not just data access -- it integrates into
     the actual work: order execution, portfolio analytics, compliance.
  4. MUSCLE MEMORY: The keyboard shortcuts, the command line -- users
     develop physical habits that make switching painful.

Why Bloomberg Is MISLEADING as a SpaceNexus Comparison:
  - Bloomberg serves 335,000 subscribers in a market of millions of
    finance professionals worldwide
  - The space industry has ~2,670 companies and ~373,000 total workers
  - Even at 100% penetration of decision-makers, the addressable market
    for a space intelligence terminal is 15,000-50,000 potential subscribers
  - Bloomberg's $14B revenue is impossible in a market this size
  - But a "Bloomberg-like" EXPERIENCE is absolutely achievable

PAYLOAD'S COMPETITIVE STRENGTHS
---------------------------------

  1. DISTRIBUTION MOAT (Most Dangerous Advantage)
     19,000+ engaged newsletter subscribers who read their content daily,
     plus separate Parallax and Polaris newsletter audiences. SpaceNexus
     has ~0 newsletter subscribers. Building a subscriber base from
     scratch takes years. Payload already has the audience that
     SpaceNexus needs to reach.

  2. BRAND RECOGNITION AND TRUST
     Space professionals know Payload by name. They are cited by SpaceNews,
     referenced in podcast discussions, and invited to speak at conferences.
     SpaceNexus is unknown outside of our direct contacts.

  3. EDITORIAL VOICE
     Human-written analysis with personality. Mo Islam's finance background
     gives credibility to business/investment analysis. Readers form
     relationships with writers -- this creates emotional switching costs.

  4. COMMUNITY ASSETS
     Executive dinners create relationships that are impossible to replicate
     digitally. Institutional members are paying for access to a network,
     not just content. This is the same model as The Information, Lenny's
     Newsletter, and other premium media businesses.

  5. REVENUE DIVERSIFICATION
     Not dependent on any single revenue stream. Advertising, subscriptions,
     events, and advisory create resilience.


PAYLOAD'S COMPETITIVE WEAKNESSES (Where SpaceNexus Wins)
---------------------------------------------------------

  1. NO REAL-TIME DATA TOOLS
     Payload cannot show you where the ISS is right now. They cannot track
     satellite constellations. They cannot display DSN communication status.
     They cannot show orbital debris density. SpaceNexus has 26+ live API
     integrations and real-time data feeds.

  2. NO INTERACTIVE CALCULATORS AND PLANNING TOOLS
     Payload has no mission cost calculator, no insurance estimator, no
     launch window planner, no link budget calculator. These are operational
     tools that professionals use daily -- not occasional reads.

  3. NO REGULATORY/COMPLIANCE FUNCTIONALITY
     Payload covers space policy editorially. SpaceNexus has actual spectrum
     management tools, regulatory filing trackers, compliance dashboards,
     and ITAR guidance. For compliance officers and regulatory affairs teams,
     this is a workflow tool, not a news source.

  4. NO API PRODUCT
     Payload has no programmatic data access. No API for developers.
     No data feeds for integration into enterprise workflows. SpaceNexus
     has a v1 API product with key management and rate limiting.

  5. NO VISUALIZATION ENGINE
     Payload has charts in their research reports, but no interactive
     visualizations, no 3D orbital views, no real-time tracking maps,
     no customizable dashboards. SpaceNexus has extensive visualization.

  6. CONTENT IS EPISODIC, NOT PERSISTENT
     A newsletter article is consumed once and buried in an inbox.
     SpaceNexus modules are persistent, always-up-to-date reference
     tools that professionals return to daily.

  7. NO COMPANY DATABASE (Yet)
     Payload Pro does not appear to have a searchable company database.
     If SpaceNexus builds the Company Intelligence Database first and
     does it well, this becomes a significant differentiator.

  8. SMALL TEAM, LIMITED ENGINEERING CAPACITY
     Payload is 5-15 people, primarily editorial. Building the kind of
     engineering-heavy features SpaceNexus has (44 modules, real-time
     data, API product, dashboard builder) would require significant
     hiring that may not align with their media-first DNA.


THE COLLISION POINT
--------------------

Both companies are converging on "the daily intelligence platform for space":

  Payload's trajectory:       Media → Research → Data → Tools
  SpaceNexus's trajectory:    Tools → Data → Content → Audience

  Timeline for head-to-head collision: 12-18 months

  Where they'll compete:
  - Both will offer "space market intelligence" dashboards
  - Both will have company profiles/analysis
  - Both will cover deals, M&A, funding
  - Both will target the same enterprise buyers

  Where they WON'T compete (our safe zones):
  - Real-time satellite tracking (Payload will never build this)
  - Regulatory compliance tools (too technical for a media company)
  - Mission planning calculators (operational, not editorial)
  - API data feeds (engineering-heavy infrastructure)
  - 3D visualization and interactive maps (engineering-heavy)


STRATEGIC RESPONSE RECOMMENDATION
-----------------------------------

PRIMARY STRATEGY: DIFFERENTIATE (Option C)
  Go deep on tools and professional features that Payload cannot easily
  replicate. Position SpaceNexus as the "operational intelligence platform"
  vs Payload's "editorial intelligence platform."

  Key message: "Payload tells you what happened. SpaceNexus shows you
  what to do about it."

  What this means tactically:
  - Prioritize the Company Intelligence Database (with operational data
    like orbital assets, contracts, spectrum -- things Payload can't match)
  - Build smart alerts that are data-driven, not editorial-driven
  - Create workflow integrations (Slack/Teams, email digests, API feeds)
  - Make compliance/regulatory tools the best in the industry
  - Build the mission planning toolkit that defense contractors need

SECONDARY STRATEGY: COMPLEMENT (Option B)
  In parallel, explore whether a partnership with Payload makes sense:
  - SpaceNexus provides data/tools that complement Payload's editorial
  - Could license our data to Payload for their research reports
  - Could cross-promote: "Analysis by Payload, data by SpaceNexus"
  - This turns a competitor into a distribution channel

  BUT: Only pursue this from a position of strength. Build the product
  first, get traction, THEN approach Payload. Coming to them as an
  unknown startup asking for a partnership will not work. Come to them
  as the platform their Pro subscribers are already using.

LONG-TERM STRATEGY: COMPETE (Option A)
  In 12-18 months, once SpaceNexus has:
  - 500+ paying customers
  - Company Intelligence Database with 500+ companies
  - Strong SEO rankings driving organic traffic
  - Regular data publications being cited by industry media

  THEN begin building editorial voice:
  - Hire 1-2 industry analysts to write original analysis
  - Launch "SpaceNexus Insights" weekly data briefing
  - Produce quarterly "State of Space" reports
  - Invite guest contributors from industry

  The endgame: SpaceNexus offers both the DATA and the ANALYSIS.
  Payload offers analysis but needs to license data from others.
  We win by being both Bloomberg (data) and Goldman Sachs Research
  (analysis).
